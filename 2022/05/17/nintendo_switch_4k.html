Playing Switch games at 4K

May 17, 2022

After working on plex-placebo, I realised that I could potentially use Anime4K for upscaling my Switch games. Turns out it works perfectly! Nowadays, you can get a 1080p60 capture card for ~AUD$120, so it‚Äôs essentially a $120 upgrade to play Switch games on your PC at up to 4K ‚Äì totally worth it imo.

TLDR: use a capture card, and use mpv to stream it with the Anime4K shaders loaded.

Instructions:

Unfortunately, I don‚Äôt have a native Linux install at the moment as I mostly use WSL (which doesn‚Äôt support USB devices without a kernel patch), so instructions are only for Windows right now.

  1. Buy a capture card that supports 1080p60. If you‚Äôre playing anything competitive or you‚Äôre sensitive to latency, I would shell out the cash for a well known, good capture card (and not the cheap $30 ones). Some of the cheaper ones lie about their specifications and only support 1080p30 or 720p60, and they have pretty bad latency e.g. ~100ms. It‚Äôs still a lot better than what the capture card market used to be just a couple of years ago though! You can find a list of popular capture cards and their latencies here.
  2. Dock your Switch
  3. Connect the HDMI to a capture card
  4. Download and install FFmpeg: https://ffmpeg.org
  5. Download mpv: https://mpv.io
  6. Follow these instructions to download and install the Anime4K mpv shaders: https://github.com/bloc97/Anime4K/blob/master/md/GLSL_Instructions_Windows.md. You can persist the shaders permanently on mpv startup by editing your mpv.conf to have glsl-shaders="~~/shaders/Anime4K_Clamp_Highlights.glsl;...".

  8. Create a shortcut to mpv.exe. Edit the shortcut via right clicking the shortcut -> Properties, and add av://dshow:video="YourVideoDevice" --profile=low-latency --untimed to the end of the Target field. The full target should look something like C:\Users\YourUsername\Documents\mpv\mpv.exe av://dshow:video="Live Gamer Ultra-Video" --profile=low-latency --untimed
  9. Unfortunately, it seems that adding the audio channel into mpv adds a significant amount of latency regardless of any low latency / no buffer settings. To get audio working, you can either:
    • Install something like Voicemeeter. Under Hardware Input 1, select your capture card‚Äôs audio device. Under Hardware Out A1, select the audio device you usually use as an output. Then, pipe the capture card‚Äôs audio to your output by clicking the ‚ÄúA‚Äù in a circle in the Hardware Input 1 column.
    • Enable loopback in your Windows settings: right click the volume icon in your system tray, and click the ‚ÄúSounds‚Äù option. Then go to the ‚ÄúRecording‚Äù tab, double click your capture card‚Äôs audio device, go to the ‚ÄúListen‚Äù tab, check ‚ÄúListen to this device‚Äù and then click apply.
  10. Power on your Switch, and run the mpv shortcut you made. You should hopefully see your Switch homepage. You can swap between the different Anime4K profiles with CTRL+1-5.

Using a 720p60 capture card

Some cheaper cards only run at 720p 60fps and 1080p 30fps. My preference is to use 720p for the higher framerates, but mpv will use the 1080p stream by default. In order to force 60fps, add the following to your shortcut: --demuxer-lavf-o=video_size=1280x720,framerate=60.

But how‚Äôs the latency?

Very good! Pretty much unnoticeable to the naked eye when comparing a passthrough HDMI vs the post-upscaled mpv stream. I had to record it using super slow-mo on my phone (960fps) to be able to see any difference. I manually counted about ~21 frames delay in the output 30fps video (700ms), so at a 960/30 = 32x slowdown, that is about 22ms of latency.

I‚Äôm not entirely sure how I have less latency than the results in the capture card comparisons I linked in Step 1 of my instructions, perhaps since mpv is running fully unbuffered? Take my measurements with a grain of salt, I only did it with my smartphone in 2 minutes (which could be doing post-processing, interpolation, etc) and I didn‚Äôt do any extensive testing with a proper lag tester.
